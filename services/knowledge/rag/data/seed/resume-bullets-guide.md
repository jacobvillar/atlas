# Writing Resume Bullets That Show Impact

Most resume bullets describe duties instead of outcomes. A stronger bullet
names the action you took, the context you took it in, and the result it
produced. A simple structure to draft from is: action verb, what you built or
changed, and the measurable or observable effect. "Maintained project
tracker" describes a duty. "Rebuilt the team's project tracker in Notion,
cutting weekly status-update time from 45 minutes to 10" describes an
outcome a reader can picture and compare against other candidates.

Numbers help, but only when they are honest and specific to what you
actually did. If you do not have a percentage or dollar figure, use a
concrete scope instead: number of users affected, size of the dataset,
number of stakeholders coordinated, or the before/after state of a process.
Avoid vague intensifiers like "significantly" or "successfully" — they add
words without adding evidence.

Lead each bullet with a strong, specific verb (built, diagnosed, negotiated,
automated, redesigned) rather than a weak opener like "responsible for" or
"worked on." Weak openers bury the action and make it harder for a reader —
human or automated — to see what you actually contributed.

Group bullets so the strongest, most relevant ones for the target role
appear first under each job. A reviewer skimming for 20 seconds should hit
your best evidence early, not after three lines of routine tasks. When you
are early-career and short on quantified wins, describe scope and
complexity instead: the number of systems touched, the ambiguity you
navigated, or the constraints you worked within.
