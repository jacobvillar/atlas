# Structuring an Early-Career Job Search

An unstructured job search often turns into applying to many postings with
little customization and no clear read on what is working. A more effective
approach treats the search like a project with a small number of target
role types, a weekly cadence, and a way to track what is and is not
converting.

Start by narrowing to two or three target role types rather than applying
broadly to anything adjacent. Broad applications usually produce generic
materials, which convert at a lower rate than a smaller number of tailored
applications. For each target role type, identify the two or three
requirements that show up most often across postings, and make sure your
resume and any portfolio work speaks to them directly.

Track applications in a simple table: company, role, date applied, source,
and status. Reviewing this weekly reveals patterns — for example, if you are
getting interviews from referrals but not cold applications, that tells you
where to invest more time. A search with no tracking makes it hard to tell
whether a lack of responses is about volume, targeting, or materials.

Treat networking as informational, not transactional: reaching out to ask
specific, genuine questions about a role or team tends to work better than
asking directly for a referral from someone you just met. People are more
willing to help when the request is small and clearly thought through.

Expect early-career searches to take longer than expected and to include
rejections that are not a reflection of overall ability — many rejections
are about fit, timing, or headcount rather than a flaw in the candidate.
Build in a regular check-in, weekly or biweekly, to reassess targeting and
materials rather than only reacting after a string of rejections.
