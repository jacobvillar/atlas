# Notes on Transitioning from a Bootcamp into a First Tech Role

Bootcamp graduates and other career changers often face a specific
challenge: they have real, demonstrable skills but a resume that looks
unconventional next to traditional computer-science backgrounds. The most
effective way to address this directly is to lead with proof of skill —
projects, contributions, and specific outcomes — rather than trying to
disguise the career change.

Frame the prior career explicitly as an asset where it genuinely is one.
Someone moving from teaching into software brought communication and
patience for debugging confusion; someone moving from operations brought
process thinking and stakeholder awareness. Naming this connection in a
resume summary or interview answer turns a potential gap into a
differentiator, but only when it is tied to something concrete the new role
actually needs.

Expect early technical interviews to weight fundamentals more heavily than
bootcamp curricula sometimes cover in depth: basic data structures, how to
reason about time complexity at a rough level, and how to debug systematically
rather than guess. Spend deliberate practice time here rather than only on
building more projects — breadth of projects with shaky fundamentals
underneath tends to stall out at the interview stage.

Be direct in applications and interviews about the bootcamp and the career
change; there is little upside in obscuring it, and being specific about
what the program covered and how you applied it independently afterward
builds more credibility than vagueness. Independent work after the program
— extending a capstone, contributing to an open-source project, or building
something unprompted — signals that the learning did not stop at
graduation, which is often exactly what a hiring manager is trying to
assess.
