# Answering Behavioral Interview Questions with the STAR Method

Behavioral interview questions ("Tell me about a time when...") ask you to
narrate a real situation rather than describe hypothetical skills. The STAR
structure — Situation, Task, Action, Result — keeps that narration focused
and easy for an interviewer to follow.

Situation sets brief context: what team, project, or constraint you were
operating under. Keep this to one or two sentences; interviewers do not need
the full backstory, just enough to understand the stakes. Task names the
specific problem or goal you were responsible for, distinct from what your
team or manager was responsible for. This is where you clarify your role,
which matters most in group projects or team efforts.

Action is the core of the answer and deserves the most detail. Describe the
specific steps you took, the decisions you made, and — importantly — why you
made them. Interviewers are listening for judgment, not just activity. If
you collaborated with others, be precise about which parts were yours.
Vague answers ("we worked together and fixed it") are harder to evaluate
than specific ones ("I proposed splitting the migration into two phases to
reduce downtime risk, then built the rollback script").

Result closes the story with the outcome, ideally something measurable or
at least clearly observable, plus a brief reflection if relevant — what you
learned or would do differently. Even for a result that did not fully
succeed, an honest account of what happened and what you took from it is
more credible than an inflated success story.

Prepare three to five STAR stories in advance covering common themes:
handling conflict, working under a tight deadline, making a mistake and
recovering, and influencing without formal authority. Most behavioral
questions map onto one of these patterns.
