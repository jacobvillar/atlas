# Tailoring a Resume to a Specific Job Description

A single generic resume rarely fits every role you apply to, because job
descriptions signal what each employer actually values, even when roles
share a title. Tailoring means adjusting emphasis and language, not
fabricating experience you do not have.

Start by reading the job description for its recurring themes: which skills
appear in multiple bullets, which tools or systems are named explicitly, and
which responsibilities are listed first (often a proxy for priority). Make a
short list of the three to five requirements that matter most for this
specific posting.

Next, scan your own experience for genuine matches — not just keyword
overlap, but real work that demonstrates the requirement. If the posting
emphasizes cross-functional collaboration and you coordinated between
design and engineering on a project, surface that explicitly rather than
leaving it implied. Reorder bullets within each role so the most relevant
evidence for this posting appears first.

Mirror the job description's terminology where it is accurate. If the
posting says "stakeholder management" and your resume says "worked with
clients," align the language — applicant tracking systems and human
reviewers both pattern-match on phrasing. Do not invent tools, metrics, or
responsibilities you did not have; misrepresenting experience creates risk
in interviews when reviewers probe for specifics.

Finally, treat your resume summary or opening line as a one-sentence pitch
for this role specifically, not a generic statement that could apply to any
job. It should read differently across applications, even if only slightly,
because it is doing different work each time.
